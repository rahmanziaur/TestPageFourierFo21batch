01 · Fundamentals

What is the Fourier Transform?

The Fourier Transform (FT) is a powerful mathematical operation that converts a signal from the spatial/time domain into the frequency domain — revealing which frequencies make up any complex waveform.

Proposed by Jean-Baptiste Joseph Fourier (1807), the transform states that any periodic signal can be expressed as a sum of sinusoids of different frequencies, amplitudes, and phases.

In image processing, a 2D image is treated as a 2D signal. The 2D Fourier Transform decomposes this image into spatial frequency components — enabling filtering, compression, restoration, and feature extraction in ways impossible in the spatial domain.

The magnitude spectrum reveals the strength of each frequency; the phase spectrum encodes structural information — together they fully describe any image.

F(u,v) = ΣΣ f(x,y) · e^(−j2π(ux/M + vy/N))
─── 2D Discrete Fourier Transform ───

f(x,y) → Spatial domain image
F(u,v) → Frequency domain representation
M, N → Image dimensions
u, v → Frequency variables

03 · Core Concepts

Key Building Blocks

Understanding these fundamental concepts is essential for mastering Fourier analysis in image processing.

🌊

Sinusoidal Basis Functions

Any signal can be expressed as a weighted sum of sine and cosine waves. These sinusoids form an orthogonal basis for signal decomposition.

Foundation
📊

Magnitude Spectrum

|F(u,v)| represents the strength of each frequency component. Low frequencies near the center correspond to smooth regions; high frequencies to edges and details.

Amplitude
⚡

Phase Spectrum

∠F(u,v) encodes the positional structure of features. Phase carries most structural information — losing phase destroys image structure even if magnitude is preserved.

Structure
⚙️

Convolution Theorem

Convolution in the spatial domain equals multiplication in the frequency domain. This reduces expensive O(n²) convolutions to fast O(n log n) FFT multiplications.

Efficiency
🚀

Fast Fourier Transform

The FFT (Cooley-Tukey, 1965) reduces computation from O(N²) to O(N log N), making real-time frequency-domain image processing possible even for large images.

Algorithm
🔄

Inverse FT (IFT)

The IFT reconstructs the spatial image from its frequency components. After filtering in the frequency domain, IFT brings the result back to the image domain.

Reconstruction

🔵 Low-Pass Filtering

Passes low frequencies, blocks high frequencies. Removes noise and fine detail — produces blurring/smoothing effect. Used in Gaussian blur.

🔴 High-Pass Filtering

Passes high frequencies, removes low. Enhances edges and fine structures. Basis for sharpening filters and edge detectors like Sobel.

🟣 Band-Pass Filtering

Selects a specific range of frequencies. Isolates textures at a particular scale. Useful for detecting periodic patterns and noise removal.

🟡 Image Compression

JPEG compression uses DCT (Discrete Cosine Transform — related to FT). Discards high-frequency coefficients that the human eye is less sensitive to.

🟢 Image Restoration

Wiener filter and blind deconvolution work in the frequency domain to remove motion blur, out-of-focus degradation, and systematic distortions.

🟠 Texture Analysis

Frequency domain reveals repeating texture patterns. Used in medical imaging (MRI, CT reconstruction), fabric inspection, and remote sensing.

07 · Mathematics

The Mathematical Framework

Core equations every signal processing engineer must know. Hover over the formula boxes for details.

Continuous FT (CFT)

F(ω) = ∫₋∞^∞ f(t) · e^(−jωt) dt

The continuous FT transforms an infinite-duration analog signal f(t) into its frequency spectrum F(ω). The complex exponential e^(−jωt) = cos(ωt) − j·sin(ωt) extracts each frequency component.

Inverse FT (IFT)

f(t) = (1/2π) ∫₋∞^∞ F(ω) · e^(jωt) dω

The IFT reconstructs the original signal from its spectrum. Both transforms are perfect inverses — no information is lost in the round trip from spatial to frequency domain and back.

2D Discrete FT (DFT)

F(u,v) = Σₓ Σᵧ f(x,y) · e^(−j2π(ux/M+vy/N))

For M×N images, this decomposes f(x,y) into frequency components F(u,v). Each (u,v) corresponds to a spatial frequency — u horizontal, v vertical — with magnitude and phase.

2D Inverse DFT

f(x,y) = (1/MN) Σᵤ Σᵥ F(u,v) · e^(j2π(ux/M+vy/N))

Reconstructs the spatial image from frequency coefficients. After applying a frequency-domain filter H(u,v), this recovers the filtered image g(x,y) = IDFT{H(u,v)·F(u,v)}.

Property Mathematical Form Significance in Image Processing
Linearity FT{af+bg} = aF+bG Superposition holds — combined images combine spectra
Convolution f*g ↔ F·G Spatial filtering = frequency multiplication (O(N log N))
Shift f(x−x₀) ↔ e^(−j2πux₀/M)F(u) Spatial shift = phase shift only; magnitude unchanged
Scaling f(ax) ↔ (1/|a|)F(u/a) Compression in space = expansion in frequency (uncertainty)
Parseval's Theorem ΣΣ|f|² = (1/MN)ΣΣ|F|² Energy is conserved between spatial and frequency domains
Separability FT{f(x)g(y)} = F(u)G(v) 2D DFT = two 1D DFTs (rows then columns) — enables fast computation
Conjugate Symmetry F(u,v) = F*(−u,−v) For real images, spectrum has Hermitian symmetry — halves storage
